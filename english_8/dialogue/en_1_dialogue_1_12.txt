Character 1: [over confidently] So you're one of those pottery experts who thinks mud spinning is some kind of spiritual journey?

Character 2: [formal] I've been working with ceramics for thirty-two years, and I assure you it's more chemistry than spirituality. Every glaze reaction follows predictable patterns based on oxide combinations and firing temperatures.

Character 1: Right, because nothing says "science" like playing with dirt.

Character 2: Clay contains aluminum silicate, iron oxide, and dozens of trace minerals that behave differently under heat stress. When you fire at cone 10, you're creating a vitrification process that transforms the molecular structure permanently.

Character 1: Sounds like you're just trying to make crafts seem important.

Character 2: The ceramic tiles on the Space Shuttle could withstand re-entry temperatures of 2,300 degrees Fahrenheit. Your coffee mug and spacecraft heat shields share the same fundamental material science—just different applications of silica-based engineering.

Character 1: [friendly] Sure, but most potters aren't exactly NASA engineers, are they?

Character 2: True, but we use the same principles. Temperature curves, thermal shock resistance, coefficient of expansion—these calculations determine whether your piece survives or explodes in the kiln.

Character 1: Explodes? Bit dramatic for a hobby.

Character 2: I've seen a trapped air bubble turn a vase into ceramic shrapnel at 2,000 degrees. The rapid expansion of heated gases doesn't care whether you call it art or engineering—physics wins every time.

Character 1: So basically you're saying pottery is dangerous. Great selling point.

Character 2: More people get injured in home kitchens than pottery studios. The difference is respect for the process—you learn quickly that clay has rules, and breaking them costs you weeks of work.

Character 1: Weeks? For a bowl? That's incredibly inefficient.

Character 2: [cheerful] Efficiency isn't always the goal! Some of my wood-fired pieces take seventy-two hours of continuous stoking to achieve the ash glazes I want. The ancient techniques create surfaces no modern method can replicate.

Character 1: Ancient techniques. Code for "we refuse to modernize."

Character 2: Actually, I use a computer-controlled kiln for most firings now. It maintains temperature curves within half a degree, preventing thermal shock better than any manual system ever could.

Character 1: Then why bother with the old methods at all?

Character 2: Because certain effects only happen through carbon trapping in reduction atmospheres or fly ash deposition in wood kilns. Technology enhances tradition—it doesn't replace the chemistry that creates truly unique surfaces.

Character 1: Unique. Another word for "inconsistent."

Character 2: Inconsistent is accurate, yes. But crystalline glazes that form differently every time, creating patterns like frozen waterfalls? That unpredictability is exactly what collectors pay thousands for—controlled chaos at 2,200 degrees.
