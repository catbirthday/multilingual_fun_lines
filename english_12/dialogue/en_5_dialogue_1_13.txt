Character 1: So you're one of those pottery experts who thinks mud spinning is some kind of spiritual journey?

Character 2: Oh man, hah, I get why you'd think that. I've been doing ceramics for, geez, thirty-something years now and honestly, it's more like... chemistry meets stubbornness? Like, every glaze reaction I do, it's basically just oxides and temperatures doing their thing, you know?

Character 1: Right, because nothing says "science" like playing with dirt.

Character 2: Okay okay, fair point, but—so clay is actually this wild mix of aluminum silicate and iron oxide and, uhh, dozens of other minerals that go absolutely bonkers under heat. When you fire at cone 10, you're essentially forcing this whole molecular restructuring that's, I mean, it's permanent. Like, forever changed.

Character 1: [confused] Sounds like you're just trying to make crafts seem important.

Character 2: Well, hmm. You know those ceramic tiles on the Space Shuttle? They could handle re-entry at like 2,300 degrees. Your coffee mug and spacecraft heat shields are basically cousins—same silica-based material science, just, you know, wildly different applications.

Character 1: Sure, but most potters aren't exactly NASA engineers, are they?

Character 2: True! God, that's so true. But we're still using the same principles, sort of. Temperature curves, thermal shock resistance, all that coefficient of expansion stuff—these calculations basically determine if your piece survives or just... explodes in the kiln.

Character 1: Explodes? Bit dramatic for a hobby.

Character 2: Oh dude, I've literally watched a trapped air bubble turn a vase into ceramic shrapnel at 2,000 degrees. The rapid expansion thing doesn't care if you call it art or whatever—physics is gonna physics, you know what I mean?

Character 1: [very sincerely] So basically you're saying pottery is dangerous. Great selling point.

Character 2: Hah, I mean, more people get hurt in their kitchens than pottery studios, honestly. The difference is like, you learn to respect the process real quick when breaking the rules means losing weeks of work. Clay doesn't forgive, essentially.

Character 1: Weeks? For a bowl? That's incredibly inefficient.

Character 2: [very sincerely] Efficiency isn't really the point though! Some of my wood-fired pieces take, geez, seventy-two hours of continuous stoking just to get those ash glazes I'm after. Those ancient techniques create surfaces that modern methods can't even touch, like, not even close.

Character 1: [stuttering] A-ancient techniques. Code for "we refuse to modernize."

Character 2: Actually, funny thing—I use a computer-controlled kiln for most of my firings now. The thing maintains temperature curves within, uhh, half a degree? It prevents thermal shock way better than any manual system ever could, which is kind of amazing when you think about it.

Character 1: Then why bother with the old methods at all?

Character 2: Because certain effects only happen through, like, carbon trapping in reduction atmospheres or when fly ash deposits in wood kilns, you know? Technology definitely enhances tradition but it can't replace the actual chemistry that creates those truly unique surfaces that just... happen.

Character 1: Unique. Another word for "inconsistent."

Character 2: [very slowly] Inconsistent is... yeah, that's accurate. But crystalline glazes that form differently every single time, creating these patterns like frozen waterfalls or something? That unpredictability is exactly what collectors pay thousands for—it's basically controlled chaos at 2,200 degrees.
