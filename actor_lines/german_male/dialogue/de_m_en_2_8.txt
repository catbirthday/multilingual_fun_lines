Character 2 [en]: The cow thing is basically just pattern recognition gone wrong. Cows lie down for dozens of reasons—they're tired, they're chewing cud, the ground feels cool—and we only remember the times when rain happened to follow. You can't really monetize something that doesn't actually work.

Character 2 [en]: Your grandmother probably developed an intuitive sense for atmospheric changes over decades of living in the same place. The real skill was hers, not some magical cow ability.

Character 1 [en]: [laugh hard] Whatever, that's hilarious, okay then what about that moon planting calendar she was obsessed with where you can only plant root vegetables during the waning moon or whatever garbage that was?

Character 2 [en]: Biodynamic planting has been around since ancient times, so you definitely can't patent it. There are some studies suggesting tidal forces might affect soil moisture and seed germination, but the effects are subtle at best. Most of the success people see is probably from the disciplined timing and attention to detail that following any calendar requires.

Character 1 [en]: God, you're being completely useless here, I need something concrete I can actually turn into profit, not these vague maybes and probablys.

Character 2 [en]: Traditional knowledge isn't like a recipe you can just extract and sell. It's this complex web of observations, stories, and practices built up over generations, and trying to pull out the profitable bits without understanding the whole system is like trying to sell a car engine without knowing what makes it run.

Character 1 [en]: She absolutely refused to teach me her water finding trick before she died, said I didn't have the right spirit or some crap like that, so can you at least explain how dowsing works so I can charge people for it?

Character 2 [en]: Dowsing is pure theater with zero scientific backing whatsoever. The successful dowsers are usually just good at reading landscape features where water naturally collects, but they don't realize they're doing it. If you want to find water, you'd be better off hiring a hydrologist than waving sticks around.

Character 1 [en]: [mocking] This is such a colossal waste of my time, I should've just sold the whole thing to those developers who called last week and been done with all this mystical nonsense.

Character 2 [en]: That would be incredibly short-sighted actually. That land contains generations of local ecological knowledge that researchers are desperately trying to preserve right now. Universities and conservation groups are literally funding projects to document this kind of traditional wisdom before it disappears forever.

Character 1 [en]: Oh please, don't give me that sanctimonious environmental lecture about preserving the past when I have real bills to pay in the present.

Character 2 [en]: I'm not being sanctimonious, I'm being practical. Traditional ecological knowledge is a legitimate field of study now, and properties with documented folk practices can qualify for grants and research partnerships. Your grandmother's methods might contain sophisticated observations about local weather patterns and soil conditions that modern science is only beginning to understand.

Character 1 [en]: Fine, if it's so valuable then tell me exactly how much these universities would pay for access to this so-called ecological knowledge.

Character 2 [en]: It doesn't work like a simple transaction where you hand over wisdom for cash. You'd need to collaborate with researchers, maybe establish the land as a study site, possibly create educational programs. The value comes from long-term partnerships, not quick sales.

Character 1 [en]: You keep talking about value but you can't give me a single concrete number, which tells me this is all just worthless sentiment dressed up as science.

Character 2 [en]: The Smithsonian paid over two million dollars for a single collection of indigenous agricultural knowledge last year. But that took years of documentation and verification, not someone trying to make a quick buck off their inheritance without understanding what they actually have.

Character 1 [en]: Whatever, I'm listing it with the realtor tomorrow because this entire conversation has been nothing but you lecturing me about things that won't pay my mortgage.

Character 2 [en]: Before you do that, you might want to check if your grandmother filed any conservation easements or historical agricultural designations. Properties with documented traditional land use often have legal protections that would make development impossible anyway, but sure, go ahead and waste everyone's time.
