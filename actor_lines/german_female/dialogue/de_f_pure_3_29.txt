Character 1: Hey, I need to understand this GDPR thing for my project. Can you help?

Character 2: Sure, GDPR is the General Data Protection Regulation from the EU. It fundamentally restructures how we handle personal data processing through six legal bases—consent, contract, legal obligation, vital interests, public task, and legitimate interests—each with distinct operational requirements.

Character 1: That sounds incredibly complicated.

Character 2: [slurring] The really complex part is Article 6 subsection 1(f) on legitimate interests. You need to conduct a three-part test balancing your interests against the data subject's fundamental rights while considering reasonable expectations—and document everything in a Legitimate Interests Assessment.

Character 1: What about cookies? Everyone talks about cookie consent.

Character 2: Cookies fall under both GDPR and the ePrivacy Directive, which creates a dual regulatory framework. You need explicit opt-in consent for non-essential cookies, granular control mechanisms for different cookie categories, and the consent must be freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous per Article 4(11).

Character 1: [bitter] This is why every website has those annoying popups now?

Character 2: Exactly, and they often implement them incorrectly. Pre-ticked boxes violate Article 7, cookie walls potentially breach the "freely given" requirement, and most fail to provide equally prominent accept and reject options as required by recent regulatory guidance.

Character 1: What happens if we mess this up?

Character 2: Penalties under Article 83 can reach 4% of global annual turnover or 20 million euros, whichever is higher. But that's for serious infringements like violating data processing principles or transferring data internationally without appropriate safeguards under Chapter V.

Character 1: International transfers? We work with US vendors.

Character 2: [pause] That's where it gets really technical. The Privacy Shield was invalidated by Schrems II, so now you need Standard Contractual Clauses plus a transfer impact assessment evaluating surveillance laws in the destination country and implementing supplementary measures where necessary.

Character 2: [sarcastic] Fantastic, because Article 37 probably requires you to appoint a Data Protection Officer if you're processing data on a large scale or conducting regular systematic monitoring. Character 2: First, map all your data flows and create Records of Processing Activities under Article 30. Then implement privacy by design per Article 25—data minimization, purpose limitation, storage limitation, pseudonymization where possible, and technical measures proportionate to risk.

Character 1: What about user rights? People keep asking about data deletion.

Character 2: [loudly] Article 17 gives them the right to erasure, but there are six exceptions! You can refuse if it's necessary for freedom of expression, legal compliance, public health, archiving in the public interest, or establishing legal claims—but you must respond within one month.

Character 1: [stern] One month? That's not much time.

Character 2: [furious] It gets worse—you can extend by two months for complex requests, but you need justified reasons!
