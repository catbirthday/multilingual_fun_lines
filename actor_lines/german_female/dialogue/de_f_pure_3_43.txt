Character 1: So what's the weirdest thing someone's brought to your sessions? Like memorabilia-wise?

Character 2: A shoebox full of movie ticket stubs from dates with an ex, arranged chronologically with notes on the back. The attachment narratives in physical objects are profound, especially when they're systematically catalogued like that.

Character 1: That's actually kind of sweet though.

Character 2: Sweet transforms to burden when you're hauling seventeen boxes of concert wristbands through three apartment moves. The material weight becomes psychological weight, and suddenly you're drowning in polyester memories from music festivals you barely remember attending.

Character 1: [joking] Seventeen boxes? What, were they going to every show in existence?

Character 2: Close enough. But here's what fascinates me—the wristbands weren't even the primary memory holders. It was the dirt still stuck in the fabric, the frayed edges from dancing, these microscopic details that held the actual emotional charge.

Character 1: That's oddly specific.

Character 2: [relieved] You'd be surprised how universal it is though. People cling to the strangest fragments—I had someone bring in a collection of fortune cookie papers, all smoothed flat and stored in a jewelry box like precious gems.

Character 1: [high-pitched] Fortune cookies? Really?

Character 2: The prophecies weren't the point. Each one marked a Chinese takeout night with their late father, Sunday tradition for eleven years. The grease stains on the paper held more meaning than any words printed on them.

Character 1: That hits different actually.

Character 2: Memory anchoring through objects follows no logical rules whatsoever. A client once couldn't throw away a broken calculator because it got them through their accounting degree, even though it hadn't worked in fifteen years.

Character 1: [enunciating every word] But isn't hoarding just hoarding sometimes?

Character 2: [condescending] Hoarding implies dysfunction, but selective preservation of meaningful objects is actually healthy memory processing. The calculator wasn't about the math—it represented surviving something they thought would break them.

Character 1: Fair point. What do you collect?

Character 2: Dried flowers from every therapy breakthrough moment, pressed between dictionary pages. When clients have major revelations, I ask if I can keep one flower from the vase on my desk, then I write the date and a single word on the dictionary page.

Character 1: That's either beautiful or creepy.

Character 2: Both can coexist. The flowers decompose eventually, turn to dust between the words, but that's the point—memories are supposed to transform, not stay frozen in amber forever.

Character 1: So you're saying we should let things decay?

Character 2: Decay is just another word for transformation. The shoebox person eventually burned all those tickets in a beach bonfire, mixed the ashes with paint, and created something entirely new—that's when memorabilia becomes medicine.
