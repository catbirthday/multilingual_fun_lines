Character 2: Right, okay, so you've got a few paths here. First thing is sending them a DMCA takedown notice—that's, uh, basically a formal legal demand that they remove your copyrighted material immediately, and most platforms actually have to comply within like 24 to 48 hours.

Character 1: How much is this going to cost me? I'm just a freelance artist, I don't have thousands for lawyers.

Character 2: Well actually, the DMCA notice itself is free to send, you can do it yourself. There are templates online that are, hmm, pretty straightforward—you just fill in your information, identify the infringed work, and boom, you send it off to their hosting provider or the platform they're selling on.

Character 1: What if they just ignore it and keep selling my stuff?

Character 2: Mmm, that's when things get more serious. If they ignore a valid DMCA, they lose their safe harbor protections which means, er, they become directly liable for copyright infringement, and at that point you could pursue damages which are, I think, anywhere from $750 to $30,000 per work infringed.
