Character 1: So I've been thinking about your whole minimalism thing. How far does it actually go?

Character 2: Pretty far, honestly. I own thirty-three items total, including clothes, and I track everything in a spreadsheet. The goal is to eliminate decision fatigue completely.

Character 1: Thirty-three items? That's wild. Do you count underwear separately or is that like one item?

Character 2: [very loudly] Each piece counts individually! Otherwise you're just lying to yourself about what you actually own. That's the whole point of the exercise.

Character 1: I love that energy! But what happens when someone gives you a gift?

Character 2: I appreciate the thought, then I donate it or regift it within twenty-four hours. Sounds harsh, but holding onto things out of guilt is exactly how clutter starts. Better to be honest upfront.

Character 1: You know what? That's actually brilliant. No emotional baggage disguised as tchotchkes.

Character 2: Exactly. The physical space reflects mental space. When everything has a specific purpose and place, your brain doesn't waste cycles on irrelevant decisions.

Character 1: Have you always been this way, or was there like a moment of awakening?

Character 2: Three years ago my apartment flooded and I lost everything. Instead of being devastated, I felt weirdly free. That's when I realized I'd been suffocating under all that stuff.

Character 1: A flood as enlightenment. That's poetic in the most chaotic way possible.

Character 2: The universe has a sense of humor. Now I could evacuate my entire life in under two minutes, laptop bag and all.

Character 1: [enunciating every word] Do you ever miss having options though? Like wanting to wear something different?

Character 2: Never. I wear the same outfit style every day—black shirt, dark jeans, same shoes. Steve Jobs had it right. Why waste mental energy on something that doesn't matter?

Character 1: [monotone] But fashion is self-expression. It's art you wear.

Character 2: Art is for walls and museums, not my closet. Self-expression happens through actions and words, not fabric choices. Plus, people remember what you say, not what you wore saying it.

Character 1: You've really thought this through. What's your endgame here?

Character 2: Financial independence by forty, then traveling with just a backpack. Most people work to buy things they don't need. I work to buy time and freedom.

Character 1: That's the most romantic thing I've heard all week, and I write wedding vows for a living.

Character 2: Romance is simple too. One person, one commitment, no games. Everything else is just noise people add because they're afraid of the silence.
