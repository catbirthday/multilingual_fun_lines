=== Original (English) ===

Character 1: Hey! Quick question about common stock - what's the real difference between voting and non-voting shares?

Character 2: [regretful] Ah, so basically voting shares give you, um, actual say in company decisions, but honestly most retail investors don't really use those rights anyway, which is kind of a shame.

Character 1: [formal] I see. And regarding dividend distributions, are they typically equivalent between the two classes?

Character 2: Yeah, usually they're the same, though, uh, some companies structure it differently. Google's Class A and C shares, for instance, they both get equal dividends but only A has voting rights, you know?

Character 1: That's wild! So why would anyone buy non-voting shares then?

Character 2: Well, I mean, they're often cheaper and more liquid since there's, like, more of them floating around. Plus, let's be real, your hundred shares aren't swaying any board elections anyway, right?

Character 1: True true! What about stock splits - do they affect both classes equally?

Character 2: [sleepily] Mmm, yeah typically they do... companies usually split all share classes proportionally to, um, maintain the existing ownership structure and avoid any weird arbitrage opportunities or whatever.

Character 1: [trailing off] And preferred stock is completely different from both of these...

Character 2: [very slowly] Oh absolutely, preferred stock is... well, it's basically a hybrid between bonds and equity, giving you fixed dividends but usually no voting rights and priority over common stockholders in bankruptcy.

Character 1: Priority in bankruptcy sounds pretty sweet actually! Why doesn't everyone just buy preferred?

Character 2: [regretful] Ah jeez, the thing is, preferred shares don't really appreciate much in value. You're basically trading growth potential for, uh, stability and those fixed payments, which isn't always the best trade-off.

Character 1: Makes sense! How about employee stock options - are those always common stock?

Character 2: Yeah so, almost always common stock because, um, companies want employees to benefit from growth. Though sometimes they'll issue restricted stock units instead which, like, vest over time but function similarly.

Character 1: RSUs! I've heard those are better tax-wise, is that true?

Character 2: Err, not exactly better, just different timing. With options you pay taxes when exercising and selling, but RSUs, hmm, you're taxed as ordinary income when they vest, regardless of whether you sell.

Character 1: Yikes, that could be a huge tax bill! Any way around that?

Character 2: Well, there's something called an 83(b) election where you can, uh, pay taxes upfront on the grant value, but it's risky because if the stock tanks or you leave before vesting, you don't get that money back.

Character 1: Brutal! Last thing - what about direct stock purchase plans versus buying on the open market?

Character 2: Oh right, DSPPs let you buy directly from the company, often at a small discount and, um, without broker fees, which is nice. But you're usually limited in how much you can buy and when you can sell, so there's definitely trade-offs there.
