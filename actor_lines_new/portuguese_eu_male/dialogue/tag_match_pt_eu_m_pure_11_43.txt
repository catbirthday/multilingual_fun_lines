Character 2 [pure]: Ter gado obriga-te a perceber de biologia, quer queiras quer não. Vetores de doença, genética, química do solo—está tudo ligado. Não consegues criar animais saudáveis em terrenos degradados, e não consegues recuperar o terreno sem perceberes o ciclo do azoto.

Character 1 [pure]: Isso é mais ciência do que a maioria usa no dia a dia.

Character 2 [pure]: Pois é, mas é aí que está. Separámos a ciência da vida quotidiana quando ela está literalmente em tudo. A fermentação na tua cerveja, os polímeros na capa do telemóvel, a combustão no motor do carro—é tudo química e física.

Character 1 [pure]: Então como é que melhoramos a literacia científica?

Character 2 [pure]: [hesitantemente] Sei lá, começar com curiosidade em vez de programas escolares? Quando as pessoas fazem perguntas sobre a vida delas—porque é que o pão cresce, como funciona o GPS—envolvem-se de forma diferente do que quando lhes despejas conceitos abstratos.

Character 1 [pure]: Mas o pessoal acredita em teorias da conspiração completamente malucas.

Character 2 [pure]: Pois.

Character 1 [pure]: Tipo o Carl Sagan ou o Neil deGrasse Tyson?

Character 2 [pure]: Exatamente como eles. Fazem-te sentir o deslumbramento de tudo aquilo, não é só decorar a tabela periódica. Quando percebes que somos literalmente feitos de pó de estrelas, a química torna-se poesia em vez de TPC.

Character 2 [pure]: Sempre que explico pastoreio regenerativo a alguém, estou a ensinar sequestro de carbono e microbiologia do solo. Só que eles nem se apercebem porque estou a falar de erva e cocó de vaca em vez de usar terminologia pomposa. É esse o segredo—desmistificar a linguagem.


=== Original (English) ===

Character 1: Do you think most people actually understand the science they quote online?

Character 2: Not really, no. Most folks are just repeating headlines they saw somewhere, maybe a Facebook post or a tweet. The actual methodology, the peer review process, the statistical significance—that all gets lost in translation.

Character 1: Even basic stuff though, like how vaccines work?

Character 2: [fungar] Vaccines are a perfect example actually. People throw around terms like herd immunity or antibodies without really grasping the mechanisms. It's like knowing a song's chorus but not understanding what the lyrics mean.

Character 1: You run cattle, right? How'd you get into following science stuff?

Character 2: Running livestock forces you to understand biology whether you want to or not. Disease vectors, genetics, soil chemistry—it's all connected. Can't raise healthy animals on degraded land, and you can't fix the land without understanding the nitrogen cycle.

Character 1: That's more science than most people use daily.

Character 2: True, but that's the problem. We've separated science from everyday life when it's literally in everything. The fermentation in your beer, the polymers in your phone case, the combustion in your car engine—it's all chemistry and physics.

Character 1: So how do we fix scientific literacy?

Character 2: [hesitantemente] Start with curiosity instead of curriculum maybe? When people ask questions about their actual lives—why does bread rise, how does GPS work—they engage differently than when you lecture them about abstract concepts.

Character 1: But people believe wild conspiracies instead.

Character 2: Yeah.

Character 1: Just yeah?

Character 2: Well, conspiracies are stories, and humans love stories. Science often gets presented as cold facts when really it's this incredible narrative of discovery and revision. We need better storytellers in science, not just better textbooks.

Character 1: You mean like Carl Sagan or Neil deGrasse Tyson?

Character 2: Exactly like them. They make you feel the wonder of it all, not just memorize the periodic table. When you understand that we're literally made of stardust, chemistry becomes poetry instead of homework.

Character 1: [alto] But how many people actually watch those shows versus scrolling TikTok?

Character 2: That's why scientists need to meet people where they are. Short videos, simple experiments, relatable examples. You can teach thermodynamics through cooking videos or evolution through dog breeding—make it relevant to what people already care about.

Character 1: Sounds like you've thought about this a lot.

Character 2: Every time I explain regenerative grazing to someone, I'm teaching carbon sequestration and soil microbiology. They just don't realize it because I'm talking about grass and cow poop instead of using fancy terminology. That's the secret—demystify the language.
