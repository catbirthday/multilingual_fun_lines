=== Original (English) ===

Character 1: Have you given any thought to solar panels for your place?

Character 2: I've been researching them for months actually. The technology has gotten so much better in the last five years, and the federal tax credits make them surprisingly affordable now.

Character 1: Really? I assumed they were still pretty expensive.

Character 2: The upfront cost can be around fifteen thousand for an average home, but with the thirty percent federal credit and state incentives, you're looking at maybe ten thousand out of pocket. Most people break even in seven to eight years.

Character 1: That's faster than I expected.

Character 2: [excité] The efficiency ratings are what blow my mind! Modern panels convert twenty-two percent of sunlight to electricity, which sounds low but it's actually incredible. The ones from just ten years ago barely hit fifteen percent efficiency.

Character 1: How much power can they actually generate though?

Character 2: A typical residential system produces about ten thousand kilowatt-hours annually, which covers most households completely. Some people even sell excess power back to the grid during peak summer months when production is highest.

Character 1: Do they work in winter?

Character 2: Absolutely.

Character 1: Even with snow?

Character 2: Snow actually reflects light which can boost output, and panels work better in cold temperatures anyway. The main issue is coverage blocking the panels, but they're usually angled steep enough that snow slides right off.

Character 1: What about maintenance?

Character 2: There's basically none. You might hose them off twice a year if you live somewhere dusty, but rain handles most cleaning naturally. The inverter might need replacing after ten years, but the panels themselves last twenty-five to thirty years easily.

Character 1: [soupir] Sounds too good to be true.

Character 2: [amer] The power companies certainly think so. They're lobbying hard against net metering policies because they're losing their monopoly. Some states are already rolling back programs that let homeowners sell power back at fair rates.

Character 1: That's frustrating.

Character 2: California just changed their rules and basically killed rooftop solar overnight. They cut the buyback rates by seventy-five percent, so now it takes twice as long to recoup your investment.

Character 1: Are there alternatives?

Character 2: Battery storage is the next frontier. Tesla Powerwalls and similar systems let you store your own power instead of selling it back. You become your own utility company, completely independent from the grid during outages.
