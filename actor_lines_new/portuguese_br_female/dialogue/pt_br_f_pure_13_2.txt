=== Original (English) ===

Character 1: Do you think everyone deserves free education?

Character 2: Oh absolutely, I mean, education is basically a fundamental human right, isn't it? The problem is, umm, we've turned it into this commodity where your zip code determines your future, which is just... it's heartbreaking.

Character 1: But who pays for it?

Character 2: Well look, we already pay for K-12 through taxes, so we've basically agreed on the principle, right? Countries like Finland and Germany manage free university education just fine—it's really about priorities, like whether we value educated citizens over, er, I don't know, tax breaks for corporations.

Character 1: My cousin dropped out because of debt.

Character 2: Jeez, that's so common it hurts. The average student loan debt is what, like thirty-seven thousand dollars now? And that's just the people who finish—imagine carrying that debt without even getting the degree.

Character 1: Is online learning the answer?

Character 2: Mmm, it helps with access for sure, but here's the thing—not everyone has reliable internet or a quiet place to study, you know? Plus you lose all that, uhh, collaborative learning and networking that happens on campus, which honestly is half the value.

Character 1: What about trade schools?

Character 2: Now that's interesting! Trade schools are actually, hmm, criminally undervalued—electricians make bank and they're not drowning in debt. We've got this weird cultural bias where we push everyone toward four-year degrees when, I mean, plenty of people would thrive in skilled trades.

Character 1: My school didn't even mention them.

Character 2: Right? That's the whole problem—guidance counselors are measured on college acceptance rates, not on whether students find sustainable careers. It's this broken system where we're basically funneling kids into debt instead of, you know, actually preparing them for life.

Character 1: [long pause] I never went to college.

Character 2: Hey, that doesn't diminish your intelligence or worth one bit, alright? Some of the smartest people I know never set foot in a university—they're self-taught or learned through experience, which is, er, equally valid.

Character 1: [very sad] Sometimes I feel so behind.

Character 2: Aah, I get that, I really do. But see, this idea that there's one timeline for education is such nonsense—people go back to school at forty, fifty, whenever, and honestly they often do better because they actually know why they're there.

Character 1: Would you hire someone without a degree?

Character 2: [cough] Absolutely, in a heartbeat if they could demonstrate the skills. The degree requirement for so many jobs is just gatekeeping, really—like, you need a bachelor's to answer phones now? It's basically class discrimination dressed up as, uhh, "qualifications."

Character 1: That's pretty radical.

Character 2: Maybe, but think about it—we're excluding brilliant people from opportunities because they couldn't afford or access formal education, which just perpetuates inequality. The whole system needs, I mean, a complete overhaul if we actually care about meritocracy.
