Character 2: Technical proficiency without soul—that was another line from your review, wasn't it? You know, I actually read all your critiques. Every single one.

Character 1: [surprised] Why would you do that to yourself? Most artists claim they never read reviews, especially the brutal ones. [quieter] And mine were definitely brutal.

Character 2: Because hidden in all that venom, there's occasionally a grain of truth. Your hatred comes from somewhere real—disappointment maybe? You clearly care about music, even if you express it through destruction.

Character 1: [bitter laugh] Don't psychoanalyze me. I hate your music because it's self-indulgent. You compose for other composers, for academics, not for actual human beings who might want to, [sighs], feel something.

Character 2: And what would you have me feel? Nostalgia for the nineteenth century? Should I write pretty melodies that don't challenge anything, that don't push any boundaries?

Character 1: [firmly] Yes!

Character 2: You don't mean that. Someone who writes with your passion, your fury—you're not actually advocating for musical wallpaper. You're angry because you think I can do better.

Character 1: [wavering] That's not—

Character 2: [gently] The third movement of my second concerto, the one you called 'deliberately obtuse'—that was about my mother's dementia. Every discordant note was another memory fragmenting. [pause] But you're right, I hid it behind technique.

Character 1: I didn't know that. You never talk about your personal life in interviews. Everything's always about theory and structure and pushing the form forward.

Character 2: [ruefully] Maybe that's my armor.

Character 1: [softer] The ending of that movement, those last thirty seconds where the chaos suddenly resolves into something almost like a lullaby—I actually played that part twice. [embarrassed laugh]

Character 2: You did? But your review said the entire piece was 'an endurance test for anyone unfortunate enough to sit through it.' I believe you used the word 'torture' three times.

Character 1: [defensive] One beautiful moment doesn't redeem forty minutes of alienating the audience. But yes, [exhales], those thirty seconds were extraordinary. They made the rest hurt more because they showed what you're capable of when you stop trying to prove how clever you are.

Character 2: [quietly moved] I'm premiering something new next month. It's different—more exposed, less architected. Maybe you'll hate it less. [soft laugh] Or maybe you'll find new reasons to destroy me.
