=== Original (English) ===

Character 1: I don't think mom recognized me today when I visited.

Character 2: [happy] Oh, sweetheart... that's—that's the disease talking, not her heart. Deep down, in places Alzheimer's can't touch? She knows exactly who you are.

Character 1: How can you be so sure?

Character 2: Because I see it happen every single day: the way her breathing changes when you walk in, how her shoulders drop that tiny bit. The body remembers what the mind sometimes... can't quite grasp.

Character 1: [very loudly] But she called me David! I'm not David!

Character 2: [sniffle] David was her brother, yes? Sometimes the brain reaches for the closest feeling-memory it can find—and you, you remind her of safety, of family, of love.

Character 1: It's getting worse every week.

Character 2: The progression isn't linear, though; some days will sparkle with clarity, others will feel... clouded. What matters is that you keep showing up, keep being present in whatever way she can receive you.

Character 1: What if she gets violent again?

Character 2: We redirect—always redirect, never confront. If she's upset about missing her mother, we don't say "your mother died forty years ago," we say "tell me about your mother."

Character 1: That feels like lying.

Character 2: [empathetically] It's not lying, love, it's... meeting her where she lives now. Forcing her to relearn painful truths every day? That would be the real cruelty.

Character 1: How do you do this every single day?

Character 2: Because every person here was somebody's everything: somebody's first love, somebody's parent, somebody's best friend. That person is still in there, just... wearing different clothes now.

Character 1: [friendly] You make it sound almost beautiful.

Character 2: Memory isn't just facts and dates—it's feeling, it's essence, it's the weight of someone's hand in yours. Those things? They're the last to go, and sometimes they never really leave.

Character 1: [sad] I miss having real conversations with her.

Character 2: [deep pitch] But you are having real conversations; they're just in a different language now. A smile, a squeezed hand, sitting together in comfortable silence—that's all real, all communication, all love.

Character 1: Will she ever have a good day again?

Character 2: Good is... relative now. But yes: there will be moments of pure joy, unexpected laughter, sudden recognition—little gifts that make everything else worth it.
